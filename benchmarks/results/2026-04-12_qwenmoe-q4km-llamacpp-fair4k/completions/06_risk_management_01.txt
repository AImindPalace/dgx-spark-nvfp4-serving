**Regime-Dependent Correlation Failure — Taleb's Correlation Instability Framework**

Taleb's work on fat-tail events emphasizes that correlation is not a stable parameter but a regime-dependent variable that explodes during crises. The normal-regime correlation of 0.28 across the 12 positions suggests moderate diversification, but Taleb's framework shows this is a dangerous illusion because:

1. Correlation is a conditional statistic — it describes relationships within a specific regime. The 0.28 figure is valid only for normal market conditions.
2. During fat-tail events, correlations converge toward 1.0 as all assets respond to the same systemic shock. The historical data showing 0.78 average pairwise correlation under VIX > 35 is the relevant regime for this situation.
3. The fact that two large volatility-targeting funds hold three of the same positions creates a forced-selling cascade risk — these funds must reduce risk by selling, creating additional downward pressure on those specific names.

**Quantifying the Volatility Difference**

Using the portfolio variance formula for N assets with equal weights:

σ²_portfolio = (1/N²) × [Σσ²_i + ΣΣρ_ij × σ_i × σ_j]

Simplified for the equal-weight case with average correlation ρ:
σ_portfolio² = (1/N) × σ²_individual + (N-1)/N × ρ × σ²_individual

Assuming an average individual position volatility of 25% annualized (reasonable for a diversified equity portfolio):

**Normal regime (ρ = 0.28):**
σ_portfolio² = (1/12) × 25² + (11/12) × 0.28 × 25²
σ_portfolio² = 52.08 + 63.83 = 115.91
σ_portfolio = √115.91 = 10.77% annualized

**Crisis regime (ρ = 0.78):**
