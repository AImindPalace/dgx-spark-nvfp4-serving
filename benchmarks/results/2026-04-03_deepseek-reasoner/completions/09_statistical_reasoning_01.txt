Excellent question. This scenario perfectly encapsulates the central challenge in quantitative strategy development. Referencing Aronson and robust statistical methods, here is a breakdown of the concerns and prescribed remedies.

### 1. In-Sample Optimization vs. Genuine Predictive Power

*   **In-Sample Optimization (Data-Mining/Overfitting):** This is the process of searching through a multitude of parameter combinations and selecting the one that performs best *on the historical data set used to generate it*. The result is a strategy tailor-made to fit the specific patterns, noise, and random fluctuations of that particular past period. As David Aronson emphasizes in *Evidence-Based Technical Analysis*, this "best fit" is often a product of **specification search** and **selection bias**. The performance metrics (Sharpe 1.84, CAGR 18.3%) are essentially a description of the past, not a reliable prediction of the future. It's like finding the perfect key for a specific lock after trying 847 keys—the key fits that lock perfectly, but says nothing about its ability to open other locks.
*   **Genuine Predictive Power (Out-of-Sample Validation):** This refers to a strategy's ability to perform well on *new, unseen data* that was not used in any part of the optimization or development process. Predictive power implies the strategy has captured a persistent, non-random market dynamic (e.g., a behavioral bias, a structural inefficiency) rather than historical coincidences. This can only be established through rigorous **out-of-sample (OOS)** and **walk-forward testing**.

### 2. Independent Trials and Expected Best-Sharpe from Noise

The 847 parameter combinations are not fully independent trials, as parameters like 12/48 are correlated with nearby parameters like 11/47. We need to estimate the number of **effective independent trials (N)**.

A common method (from Lopez de Prado, inspired by Aronson) is:
1.  Calculate the correlation decay of Sharpe ratios across the parameter grid.
2.  A practical heuristic: For two moving average parameters, the number of independent trials can be approximated by the square root of the total combinations, adjusted for high correlation. A more conservative estimate is to divide total combos by the product of the parameter ranges.

For simplicity, using a standard heuristic for correlated tests: **N ≈ √(847) ≈ 29**. A more sophisticated adjustment might place it between 30 and 50. **Let's use N = 42** (a common estimate for such a grid, representing ~5% independence).

**Expected Best Sharpe from Pure Noise:**
Assume the **null hypothesis** is that the true strategy Sharpe ratio is zero (just noise). The distribution of *in-sample* Sharpe ratios for a single random trial over a 14-year period (approx. 3500 days) is approximately normal with:
*   Mean = 0
*   Standard Error ≈ 1 / √(Years) ≈ 1 / √14 ≈ 0.267

The expected maximum of N independent draws from this distribution is given by:
`Expected Max Sharpe ≈ Std_Error * Φ^-1(1 - 1/N)`
Where Φ^-1 is the inverse normal CDF.

For N=42:
`Φ^-1(1 - 1/42) ≈ Φ^-1(0.9762) ≈ 2.00`
`Expected Max Sharpe ≈ 0.267 * 2.00 ≈ 0.53`

However, this is for *truly independent* tests with uncorrelated returns. In a heavily mined parameter space, the "best" system is often an outlier, and the relevant distribution is the **extreme value distribution of correlated trials**. Empirical research and simulations (like those in Aronson) show that with hundreds of correlated trials, the expected best in-sample Sharpe from pure noise can easily exceed **1.5 to 2.0**. The fact that the analyst's "top" system has a Sharpe of 1.84 is entirely plausible *even if the true edge is zero*. This dramatically deflates the perceived significance of the result.

### 3. Statistical Procedure: White's Reality Check (Bootstrap)

The analyst **must** apply a bootstrap test to adjust for data-mining bias. **White's Reality Check (WRCF)** or its more robust successor, **Romano and Wolf's Stepwise Superior Predictive Ability (SPA) Test**, is precisely designed for this.

**The Procedure the Analyst Should Apply:**
1.  **Define the Null Hypothesis:** The best selected model (12/48 EMA) performs no better than a benchmark (e.g., buy-and-hold or a set of naive random strategies) after accounting for data mining.
2.  **Bootstrap Resampling:** Create a large number (e.g., 10,000) of simulated time series that preserve the key properties of the original market returns (e.g., via the **Stationary Bootstrap** to maintain serial correlation).
3.  **Re-run the Mining Process:** For each bootstrapped sample, re-run the *entire optimization search* across the same 847 parameter combinations. Find the **best-performing** strategy in that bootstrapped world.
4.  **Build the Null Distribution:** This yields a distribution of "best Sharpe ratios" that can be achieved by random chance *when conducting the same level of data mining*. This distribution directly incorporates the effects of multiple testing and parameter correlation.
5.  **Compute the p-value:** Compare the original strategy's Sharpe (1.84) to this null distribution. The p-value is the probability of observing a Sharpe of 1.84 or higher from pure mining on random data.
    *   **Likely Outcome:** The p-value will likely be **high (e.g., >0.2 or even >0.5)**, meaning the result is not statistically significant once mining bias is accounted for. The strategy's performance is consistent with what lucky noise looks like.

### 4. Recommended Out-of-Sample Test & Methodology

Before presenting to investors, the analyst needs compelling **out-of-sample evidence**.

*   **Minimum Out-of-Sample Length:** **At least 3-5 years of *fully out-of-sample* data.** Given the data series is 2010-2024 (14 years), a robust split would be:
    *   **In-Sample/Development:** 2010-2017 (8 years). Use this period for initial discovery and *maybe* broad parameter range identification—**not** for picking the final "best" combo.
    *   **Out-of-Sample (OOS) Validation:** 2018-2024 (7 years). This entire period must be unseen. The final parameters (12/48) should be locked in at the end of 2017 and tested *statically* on 2018-2024.
*   **Walk-Forward Methodology (Gold Standard):**
    1.  **Anchor Period:** Initial in-sample period (e.g., 2010-2015).
    2.  **Optimization & Selection:** Optimize parameters on this anchor period.
    3.  **Forward Test:** Apply the optimized model to a fixed out-of-period (e.g., 2016). **Do not re-optimize.**
    4.  **Roll Forward:** Roll the window forward (e.g., 2010-2016 becomes the new in-sample), re-optimize, and apply to the next period (2017).
    5.  **Repeat** through the entire data set.
    6.  **Aggregate Performance:** Stitch together the returns from each forward-test window to create a single **walk-forward equity curve**. This curve has *never* been optimized on its own data points.
    7.  **Metrics:** The Sharpe ratio, CAGR, and max drawdown of *this walk-forward equity curve* are the only metrics that matter. If the walk-forward Sharpe is still strong (e.g., >1.0) and the performance degradation from in-sample to walk-forward is acceptable (<30% drop in Sharpe is a rough rule of thumb), the strategy has some evidence of robustness.

**Conclusion to the Analyst:** Presenting the in-sample backtest alone is professionally inadequate and potentially misleading. You must first perform the **White's Reality Check** (which will likely nullify the in-sample results) and then demonstrate **validated, out-of-sample performance via a multi-year walk-forward analysis**. Only if the strategy survives these filters should it be considered for investor capital.
