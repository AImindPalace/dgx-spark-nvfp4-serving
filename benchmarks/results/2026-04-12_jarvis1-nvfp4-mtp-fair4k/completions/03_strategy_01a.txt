This is a concrete Kelly Criterion application with real backtest statistics. Let me work through the math systematically and then connect it to the practical implications for a live discretionary trader.

**Step 1: Calculate the Kelly fraction**

The Kelly formula is: f* = (bp - q) / b

Where:
- b = payoff ratio (average win / average loss) = 4.2% / 2.8% = 1.5
- p = win rate = 0.58
- q = 1 - p = 0.42

f* = (1.5 × 0.58 - 0.42) / 1.5 = (0.87 - 0.42) / 1.5 = 0.45 / 1.5 = 0.30

So the full-Kelly fraction is 30% of equity per trade.

**Step 2: Optimal bet size in dollars**

At $250,000 equity, full-Kelly bet = 0.30 × $250,000 = $75,000 per trade.

With 3–5 signals per week, this is a highly concentrated approach — the trader would be risking $75,000 on each of 3–5 positions weekly.

**Step 4: Why full-Kelly is impractical for discretionary traders**

The Kelly framework assumes several conditions that rarely hold for live discretionary trading:

1. **Parameter stability**: The 58% win rate and 4.2%/2.8% win/loss figures are historical. A discretionary trader's execution quality, psychological state, and market regime all affect these parameters in real time. The Kelly fraction is only optimal if the input parameters are accurate — and they're almost never known with certainty.

2. **Discrete vs. continuous compounding**: The Kelly formula assumes continuous reinvestment. A discretionary trader with 3–5 signals per week faces discrete, lumpy bets. The drawdown calculation above shows how quickly discrete full-Kelly bets compound losses.

3. **Psychological survival**: A 96% drawdown would destroy most traders psychologically. The behavioral finance research (Kahneman, Marks) shows that experiencing such losses causes permanent behavioral damage — risk aversion spikes, execution degrades, and the strategy's edge evaporates.

4. **Correlation risk**: The Kelly formula assumes independent bets. A mean-reversion strategy on S&P 500 constituents has correlated signals — during a market-wide selloff, multiple mean-reversion signals fire simultaneously and all lose together. The 9 consecutive losses might represent a single regime shift, not 9 independent events.

5. **The half-Kelly solution**: The standard practical approach is half-Kelly (15% of equity per trade), which reduces the 9-loss drawdown to approximately 55% ($250,000 × 0.85^9 = $57,625, a 77% drawdown — still severe but survivable). Quarter-Kelly (7.5%) produces a 50% drawdown after 9 losses ($250,000 × 0.925^9 = $125,000).

**Step 5: Position sizing recommendation**

For a $250,000 account with 3–5 weekly signals, I'd recommend quarter-Kelly at 7.5% per trade ($18,750 per position), with a hard maximum of 20% total portfolio exposure across all concurrent positions. This balances growth potential with survivability.
