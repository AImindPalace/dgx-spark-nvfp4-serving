**Step 2: Bayesian updating framework (Aronson's approach)**

Aronson's Bayesian method requires: (a) specifying a prior probability, (b) computing the likelihood of the observed evidence under both the thesis being true and false, and (c) updating to a posterior probability.

Let's formalize:
- H = hypothesis that the USD short thesis is correct (US weakens, EUR strengthens)
- P(H) prior = 0.65 (subjective prior confidence)
- E = the new evidence (strong NFP, wage acceleration, GDPNow revision, Fed cut repricing)

Likelihoods:
- P(E|H) = probability of seeing this strong data IF the thesis is correct. If US growth is decelerating and the Fed is cutting, we should NOT see +312,000 NFP, +4.1% wage growth, and 3.4% GDPNow. This likelihood is very low — perhaps 0.10.
- P(E|¬H) = probability of seeing this strong data IF the thesis is WRONG. If US growth is actually accelerating and the Fed is holding, this data is exactly what we'd expect. Likelihood: perhaps 0.85.

Bayes' theorem: P(H|E) = [P(E|H) × P(H)] / [P(E|H) × P(H) + P(E|¬H) × P(¬H)]
P(H|E) = [0.10 × 0.65] / [0.10 × 0.65 + 0.85 × 0.35] = 0.065 / [0.065 + 0.2975] = 0.065 / 0.3625 = 0.179

The posterior probability of the thesis drops from 65% to approximately 18%. This is a dramatic Bayesian update — the evidence strongly favors the alternative hypothesis.
