**The Grossman-Stiglitz Resolution: Markets Are Efficient Enough, But Not Perfectly So**

The paradox's resolution is that markets exist on a spectrum of efficiency. The equilibrium is a state where the marginal cost of information acquisition equals the marginal benefit of the information — meaning some inefficiencies persist precisely because eliminating them would be too costly. This means genuine alpha exists but is concentrated in specific conditions:

1. **Illiquid or structurally constrained markets** where institutional capital cannot operate (small-cap equities, emerging market debt, niche derivatives)
2. **Behavioral inefficiencies** that persist because they exploit systematic cognitive biases (anchoring, herding, loss aversion) that are not arbitraged away
3. **Microstructural inefficiencies** in execution (latency arbitrage, order flow prediction) that require specialized infrastructure
4. **Regime-specific inefficiencies** that appear during stress periods when liquidity providers withdraw

**Aronson's Epistemological Framework: The Burden of Proof**

Aronson's central argument is that the burden of proof lies with the strategy proponent. A strategy must demonstrate:
- Out-of-sample statistical significance (p < 0.05 after correcting for multiple testing)
- Risk-adjusted returns exceeding the cost of capital and transaction costs
- Robustness across different market regimes
- No evidence of data-mining bias (validated through White's Reality Check or similar methods)

The minimum threshold for genuine alpha: a Sharpe ratio of at least 1.0 on out-of-sample data, sustained over a minimum of 2 years, with transaction costs fully deducted. Anything below this is indistinguishable from chance.

Dalio's principle of radical truth-seeking means the portfolio manager must treat every strategy as a hypothesis to be tested, not a belief to be defended. The actionable framework: allocate research resources proportionally to the strategy's demonstrated edge and half-life.
