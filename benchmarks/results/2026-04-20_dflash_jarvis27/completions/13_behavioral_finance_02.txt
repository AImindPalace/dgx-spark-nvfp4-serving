This is a textbook case of the disposition effect — the behavioral tendency to sell winners too early and hold losers too long — and it perfectly illustrates how prospect theory's asymmetric utility function destroys trading profitability even when the win rate appears healthy.

The data tells a devastating story: 62% win rate sounds excellent, but the average winner of $420 versus the average loser of $890 produces a negative expectancy of -$77 per trade. The holding time asymmetry is the smoking gun — winners are held 4.2 days on average while losers are held 11.7 days, a 2.8x ratio. This means the trader is cutting profits short and letting losses run, which is the exact inverse of what profitable trading requires.

The 14 trades where he took profits that subsequently ran 15–30% further illustrate the disposition effect's cost. Each of those premature exits represents not just the foregone gain but also the psychological reinforcement of the pattern — seeing a stock continue higher after selling it creates regret, which in turn makes the trader more anxious to lock in the next winner early.

Applying the prospect theory framework: the utility function is concave for gains (diminishing marginal utility — the 10th dollar of profit feels less valuable than the 1st) and convex for losses (increasing marginal disutility — the 10th dollar of loss feels more painful than the 1st). The loss aversion coefficient of approximately 2.0–2.5x means a $1 loss feels as bad as a $2–$2.50 gain feels good. This explains the holding asymmetry: the trader sells winners because the concave utility curve makes each additional dollar of profit feel less worth the risk of giving it back, while he holds losers because the convex loss curve makes realizing the loss feel catastrophically worse than continuing to hope for recovery.

The disposition effect framework from Shefrin and Statman, as Marks synthesizes it, identifies this as a systematic behavioral error that is correctable through structured protocols. The key insight is that the trader's emotional decision-making is predictable and consistent — which means it can be overridden by rules.

The correction protocol must address both sides of the asymmetry simultaneously. For the winner side: implement a trailing stop method or a minimum target rule that forces the trader to let winners run. For the loser side: implement a hard stop-loss rule that forces exit before the loss becomes psychologically unbearable. The specific parameters should be calibrated to the trader's historical data.

Concrete protocol design: (1) Every trade must have a pre-defined stop-loss at entry, maximum 1.5x the average winner size ($630), which means the maximum loss per trade is capped at $630 — this immediately eliminates the $890 average loser. (2) Every trade must have a minimum target of at least 2:1 risk-reward, meaning if the stop is $630, the target must be at least $1,260. (3) Implement a trailing stop rule: once a trade is up 1R ($630), move the stop to breakeven; once up 2R ($1,260), trail the stop at 1R below the highest price. (4) The 14 premature exits that ran 15–30% further suggest the trader's targets are too conservative — he should use a trendline or resistance-based target method rather than a fixed percentage.

The expected outcome of this protocol: if the trader maintains his 62% win rate but caps losers at $630 and allows winners to reach at least $1,260 (2:1), expectancy becomes (0.62 × $1,260) - (0.38 × $630) = $781 - $239 = +$542 per trade. Even if the win rate drops to 55% due to the wider targets, expectancy is (0.55 × $1,260) - (0.45 × $630) = $693 - $284 = +$409 per trade. The protocol transforms a -$77 expectancy into a +$400–$540 expectancy.
