The critical insight is that the equilibrium point shifts: as more participants adopt passive strategies, the remaining inefficiencies become more valuable, attracting more active capital, which erodes those inefficiencies. This is a dynamic equilibrium, not a static one.

**Conditions for Genuine Alpha — A Four-Filter Model:**

Drawing on Aronson's epistemological framework, I propose four necessary conditions that any alpha claim must satisfy:

1. **Identifiable inefficiency:** The strategy must target a specific, named market inefficiency (e.g., 'small-cap post-earnings drift,' 'momentum in illiquid markets,' 'behavioral anchoring in retail-heavy stocks'). Vague claims like 'I read charts better' fail this filter.

2. **Mechanism plausibility:** There must be a credible economic or behavioral mechanism explaining why the inefficiency persists. The Grossman-Stiglitz model tells us inefficiencies persist when the cost of exploiting them exceeds the profit opportunity for most participants. Your mechanism must explain why this particular inefficiency survives.

3. **Survival of falsification:** Following Aronson's method, the strategy must have been tested against a rigorous null hypothesis with proper out-of-sample validation. A minimum standard: at least 5 years of backtesting, 2 years of out-of-sample testing, and a p-value below 0.05 after correcting for multiple testing.

4. **Capacity-awareness:** The edge must be large enough relative to the fund's size. A strategy generating 3% annual alpha on $5M is trivially exploitable; the same 3% on $5B requires $150M in annual alpha, which demands a fundamentally different edge.

**Scale-Specific Research Intensity:**

For the $5M discretionary trader: Research intensity should be high on microstructure and behavioral inefficiencies. Small-cap stocks, illiquid markets, and retail-dominated sectors contain inefficiencies that are invisible to large funds. For the $5B quant fund: Research intensity must be extreme on data science and execution. The edge is in speed, breadth, and cost minimization. Research time: 50-100 hours/week distributed across a team of 20-50 researchers. The edge is in processing power and data access, not individual insight.
