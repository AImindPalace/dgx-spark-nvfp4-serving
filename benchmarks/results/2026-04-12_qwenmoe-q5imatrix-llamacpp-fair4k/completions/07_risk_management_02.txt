**Current Situation Analysis:**

The fund is at -11.2% drawdown with a 15% hard limit — it has 3.8% of drawdown capacity remaining. At $480M AUM, that's $18.24M of remaining risk tolerance. The portfolio heat of 8.3% means the fund is currently risking $39.84M (8.3% × $480M) in open positions. This is already 2.18x the remaining drawdown capacity — a dangerous mismatch that DePrado's HRP framework would flag immediately.

The 40% expansion in ATR above the 20-day average is a critical signal. Under the volatility-targeting model, position sizes should scale inversely to realized volatility. If volatility has increased 40%, the model is calling for positions to be sized at approximately 1/1.40 = 71.4% of their normal size. But the fund is running at 8.3% heat — meaning it's actually running positions that are oversized relative to current volatility conditions.

**DePrado's HRP Framework Application:**

DePrado's hierarchical risk parity method addresses exactly this problem through its covariance-based clustering approach. The key insight is that HRP doesn't just allocate capital — it allocates risk. When volatility expands, HRP's recursive bisection algorithm automatically reduces position sizes across all clusters because the covariance matrix entries increase, and the risk contribution of each asset rises.

The operational procedure should be:

1. **Immediate (today):** Calculate the volatility-adjusted portfolio heat. If the 8.3% heat was calculated using normal volatility, the true volatility-adjusted heat at 1.40x volatility is approximately 8.3% × 1.40 = 11.62%. This is dangerously close to the 15% limit.

2. **Apply the volatility scaling rule:** Reduce all position sizes to 71.4% of their current levels. This brings portfolio heat from 8.3% to approximately 5.93% (8.3% × 0.714). This creates a buffer of 9.07% below the 15% limit — a 2.39x safety margin.

3. **Hierarchical clustering reassessment:** The expanded volatility likely changes the correlation structure. Some clusters that were previously distinct may now show higher intra-cluster correlations, requiring further risk reduction within those clusters.

**Stop-Out Threshold:**

The fund should implement a dynamic stop-out rule based on the remaining drawdown capacity. At -11.2% drawdown with 3.8% remaining, and given that the volatility expansion means each point of portfolio heat now represents more dollar risk, the stop-out should trigger at portfolio heat of 5.0% (not the current 8.3%). This provides a 2.5x buffer between current heat and the stop-out level.

In dollar terms: 5.0% × $480M = $24M maximum open risk. If the fund's risk model estimates that the current positions could lose $24M under stressed conditions, liquidate immediately.

**Operational Risks of Liquidation into Elevated Volatility:**

This is the most critical practical concern. DePrado's work on hierarchical risk parity emphasizes that the covariance matrix is the foundation of the entire framework — and covariance is most unstable during periods of elevated volatility. Liquidating into a 40% ATR expansion creates several risks:

1. **Slippage amplification:** Selling into elevated volatility means wider bid-ask spreads and larger market impact. A $100M liquidation that would normally cost 5 basis points in slippage could cost 12-15 basis points in this environment.

2. **Correlation breakdown:** During volatility spikes, correlations tend to converge toward 1.0 across asset classes. This means the diversification benefit that HRP relies on temporarily disappears, and the fund's risk model may underestimate true portfolio risk.

3. **Fire-sale dynamics:** If the fund is forced to liquidate because it hits the 15% limit, it will be selling into the worst conditions — exactly when other funds are also liquidating. This creates a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

4. **Re-entry difficulty:** After liquidating, re-establishing positions at normal levels requires volatility to normalize. If the fund liquidates at -13% drawdown and volatility takes 3 weeks to normalize, the fund is sitting in cash for 3 weeks while missing potential recovery.

The operational rule: Never liquidate more than 50% of any single position in a single day during elevated volatility. Use limit orders at mid-price rather than market orders. If the fund must reduce heat from 8.3% to 5.0%, do it over 2-3 days, not 1 day.
