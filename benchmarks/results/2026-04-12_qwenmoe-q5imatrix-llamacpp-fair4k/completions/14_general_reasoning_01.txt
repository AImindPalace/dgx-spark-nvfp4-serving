The Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox is the key theoretical anchor. It states that if markets were perfectly informationally efficient, no one would have an incentive to gather information (because it would be worthless), but if no one gathers information, markets cannot be efficient. Therefore, markets must be somewhere between perfectly efficient and perfectly inefficient — a state of "noisy efficiency" where some information is reflected in prices and some is not. This means active management is not futile, but it is also not guaranteed to succeed. The paradox implies that the degree of inefficiency is endogenous — it depends on how many participants are actively searching for alpha.

Dalio's framework adds a crucial epistemological layer. In his principles, he emphasizes that the most important thing is to understand reality as it is, not as you wish it to be. This means that the question isn't "is active management possible?" but "under what specific conditions does active management work, and how do I know I'm in those conditions?" Dalio's method of radical truth-seeking means you must test your alpha-generating hypotheses against reality, not against the comforting narrative that your edge is real.

Aronson's work on the limits of technical analysis provides the methodological framework for distinguishing real from illusory alpha. His research shows that most technical patterns fail to generate statistically significant excess returns after transaction costs. The key insight from Aronson is that you need to test your edge against a large enough sample of data, with proper out-of-sample validation, and with realistic cost assumptions. If your backtest doesn't survive these tests, your edge is illusory.
