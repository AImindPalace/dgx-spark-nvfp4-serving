**In-Sample vs. Genuine Predictive Power**

Aronson explains that in-sample optimization is the process of finding the parameter combination that would have produced the best historical results. This is fundamentally different from genuine predictive power, which is the ability to produce good results on data the model has never seen. The critical insight is that when you search through many parameter combinations, you are guaranteed to find at least one that looks good — even if all parameters are random noise.

Aronson provides a concrete example: if you test 100 random parameter combinations, you should expect the best one to have a Sharpe ratio of approximately 0.5–0.7 just from random chance. With 847 combinations, the expected best-Sharpe from pure noise is substantially higher.

**Estimating Independent Trials**

The key question is: how many independent trials does 847 parameter combinations represent? This is not 847 — many combinations are highly correlated. Two EMA pairs that are close together (e.g., 12/48 and 13/49) will produce nearly identical results. A rough rule of thumb is that independent trials are approximately the square root of the total combinations when parameters are smoothly varying, so √847 ≈ 29 independent trials.

Using Aronson's framework for the expected best-Sharpe from random data, with approximately 29 independent trials, the expected best-Sharpe from pure noise is approximately 0.8–1.0. The observed 1.84 is higher, but not dramatically so — it could easily be a statistical fluke.

**White's Reality Check**

Aronson describes White's Reality Check (also called the bootstrap method) as the proper statistical procedure. The procedure is:

1. Generate 1,000+ random parameter combinations (not just the 847 tested)
2. For each random combination, compute the Sharpe ratio
3. Find the best Sharpe ratio among all random combinations
4. Repeat steps 1–3 many times (1,000+ iterations)
5. Build a distribution of best-Sharpe values from pure noise
6. Compare the actual best-Sharpe (1.84) to this distribution

If the actual best-Sharpe exceeds the 95th percentile of the noise distribution, the strategy has genuine predictive power at the 5% significance level. If it's below, the results are likely due to data mining.

**Walk-Forward and Out-of-Sample Requirements**

Aronson's framework for walk-forward testing requires:

1. **Out-of-sample test length**: At least 1 year of daily data (252 trading days) for a daily strategy. For a strategy with a 12/48 EMA crossover, the average trade duration is approximately 30 days, so 252 days provides roughly 8 independent trade cycles.

2. **Walk-forward methodology**: Divide the data into training and testing windows that roll forward. For example:
   - Train on 2010–2015 (6 years)
   - Test on 2016–2017 (2 years)
   - Retrain on 2010–2017
   - Test on 2018–2019
   - Retrain on 2010–2019
   - Test on 2020–2024 (5 years)

3. **Minimum performance threshold**: The out-of-sample Sharpe should be at least 0.7 (roughly half the in-sample Sharpe) to be considered viable. The 2018–2024 period includes the 2020 COVID crash, which is a critical stress test.

4. **Parameter stability**: The optimal parameters should not change dramatically between training windows. If the 12/48 combination works in 2010–2015 but the optimal parameters shift to 8/32 or 20/60 in later windows, the strategy is overfit to specific market regimes.

**Recommendation**

The analyst should not present the 1.84 Sharpe ratio to investors without first running the White's Reality Check and walk-forward analysis. The strategy may be viable, but the current evidence is insufficient to distinguish genuine edge from data-mining artifact.
