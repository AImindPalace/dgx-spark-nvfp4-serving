The trader has a mean-reversion strategy with a 58% win rate, +4.2% average winner, -2.8% average loser, and 9 consecutive losses as the maximum observed. The Kelly Criterion provides a framework for sizing, but its application requires careful interpretation.

**Full Kelly Calculation:**

The Kelly formula for a binary bet is: f* = (p × b - q) / b, where p = win probability, q = loss probability (1 - p), and b = net odds received (win amount / loss amount).

For this strategy:
- p = 0.58
- q = 0.42
- b = 4.2 / 2.8 = 1.5 (the average win is 1.5 times the average loss)

f* = (0.58 × 1.5 - 0.42) / 1.5 = (0.87 - 0.42) / 1.5 = 0.45 / 1.5 = 0.30

Full Kelly suggests risking 30% of the account per trade.

On a $250,000 account, that's $75,000 per trade.

**Drawdown During the 9-Trade Losing Streak:**

With 30% risk per trade and 9 consecutive losses, the account compounds down:

After 1 loss: $250,000 × 0.70 = $175,000
After 2 losses: $175,000 × 0.70 = $122,500
After 3 losses: $122,500 × 0.70 = $85,750
After 4 losses: $85,750 × 0.70 = $60,025
After 5 losses: $60,025 × 0.70 = $42,018
After 6 losses: $42,018 × 0.70 = $29,413
After 7 losses: $29,413 × 0.70 = $20,589
After 8 losses: $20,589 × 0.70 = $14,412
After 9 losses: $14,412 × 0.70 = $10,088

The account is down 96% from $250,000 to $10,088. This is catastrophic.

Even if we use a more realistic position sizing method where the Kelly fraction represents the fraction of the account at risk (not the fraction of the account being traded), the drawdown is still severe. If the trader risks 30% of the account per trade (meaning 30% of the current equity is at risk on each trade), the drawdown compounds similarly.

**Why Full Kelly Is Impractical:**

1. **Parameter uncertainty:** The 58% win rate and 4.2% average winner are estimated from 5 years of data. These parameters have standard errors. The Kelly formula is extremely sensitive to parameter errors — a 1% overestimate of the win rate can double the recommended Kelly fraction. As Taleb emphasizes in his work on fragility, the Kelly Criterion assumes known probabilities, but in practice we only have estimates.

2. **Fat-tail risk:** The 9 consecutive losses observed over 5 years (roughly 780 trading days) is a sample of one. The true probability of a 10-trade or 12-trade losing streak may be higher than the sample suggests. Kelly sizing doesn't account for the possibility that the observed maximum streak is not the true maximum.

3. **Psychological impossibility:** A discretionary trader watching their account drop from $250,000 to $10,088 during a losing streak would almost certainly abandon the strategy mid-run. The behavioral finance literature on loss aversion (Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory) shows that losses hurt roughly 2.25 times more than equivalent gains feel good. A 96% drawdown would trigger panic selling and strategy abandonment.

4. **Kelly's own recommendation:** Even William T. Thomson, who formalized the Kelly Criterion, recommended using half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly in practice. Half-Kelly for this strategy would be 15% risk per trade, which still produces a drawdown of approximately 77% over 9 consecutive losses ($250,000 → $58,594). Quarter-Kelly (7.5% risk) produces a drawdown of approximately 49% ($250,000 → $127,500), which is painful but survivable.

5. **The Calmar ratio of 1.4 is modest:** A Calmar ratio of 1.4 means the strategy's average annual return is 1.4 times its maximum drawdown. This suggests the strategy's maximum drawdown over the 5-year period was likely 30–40% of annual returns. Full-Kelly sizing would amplify this drawdown by a factor of roughly 2–3x compared to the backtest's implied sizing, making the live experience far worse than the backtest suggests.

Use quarter-Kelly (7.5% risk per trade) or even half-Kelly (15% risk per trade) with a hard maximum position size of $30,000 (12% of account) regardless of the Kelly calculation. This provides a reasonable risk-reward while keeping the psychological burden within survivable bounds. The trader should also implement a maximum drawdown circuit breaker — if the account drops 25% from peak ($187,500), the strategy should be paused for review.
