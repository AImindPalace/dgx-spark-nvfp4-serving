**Step 2: Orient — The most critical and bias-prone phase.**
This is where Boyd's framework is most powerful and where cognitive biases are most dangerous. The trader's original thesis (long breakout continuation) was formed before the open based on yesterday's close. The Orient phase requires updating the mental model with new information. Common biases that threaten this phase:

- **Confirmation bias**: The trader may subconsciously search for evidence supporting the original thesis while ignoring the bearish engulfing bar and the Fed comment.
- **Anchoring**: The trader may anchor to the pre-open expectation of a breakout and treat the reversal as a temporary pullback rather than a regime change.
- **Sunk cost fallacy**: Having already prepared the trade, the trader may feel committed to executing it.
- **Framing bias**: The trader may frame the reversal as "a pullback in a bull market" rather than "a potential trend reversal."

McChrystal's work emphasizes that in complex environments, the Orient phase must be continuous — not a one-time event. The trader must ask: "What has changed since I formed my thesis?" The answer is everything: price action has reversed, and the macro catalyst has shifted.

Given the alignment of price and macro signals, Option 2 (short) has the best risk-reward. However, the trader should wait for confirmation — a break below the engulfing bar's low — before entering.

**Step 4: Act — Execute with discipline.**
The trader should not hesitate. Boyd's framework emphasizes speed of cycling through the OODA loop as a competitive advantage. The trader who cycles faster than the market adapts to new information first.
