**Step 2: Assess the upward bias in small-sample Sharpe estimates**

Aronson's framework identifies several mechanisms that cause small-sample Sharpe ratios to be upward-biased:

1. **Selection bias**: The trader likely has a long history of strategies, and this is the one that survived 63 days. If they tested 10 strategies and only the best one was deployed, the sample Sharpe is inflated by the selection process.

2. **Survivorship bias**: The strategy has survived 63 days without hitting a drawdown large enough to trigger a stop or cause the trader to abandon it. Strategies that would have failed are no longer in the sample.

3. **Regression to the mean**: A Sharpe of 2.71 over 63 days is statistically extreme. The probability of observing a Sharpe this high from a strategy with a true Sharpe of 0 is essentially zero. Even from a strategy with a true Sharpe of 1.0, the probability is very low. This means the true Sharpe is almost certainly lower than 2.71.

4. **Data-mining bias**: If the strategy was developed through iterative optimization (as most retail strategies are), the 63-day sample is the continuation of a data-mining process, not an independent test.

**Step 4: Type I and Type II error risks**

**Type I error (false positive)**: The trader concludes the strategy has a genuinely high Sharpe and scales up, when in reality the true Sharpe is low or negative. The probability of this error depends on the true Sharpe. If the true Sharpe is 0, the probability of observing a sample Sharpe of 2.71 over 63 days is vanishingly small (p < 0.001). If the true Sharpe is 1.0, the probability is still low but non-negligible. The risk is that the trader is seeing a statistical fluke amplified by selection bias.

**Type II error (false negative)**: The trader concludes the strategy is not reliable and does not scale up, when in reality the true Sharpe is high. This is the less dangerous error for a retail trader, because the cost of waiting is opportunity cost, not capital loss.

The asymmetry of these errors is critical: the Type I error (scaling up a false-positive strategy) can destroy capital, while the Type II error (not scaling a true-positive strategy) costs only opportunity. This asymmetry means the trader should use a conservative decision rule.
