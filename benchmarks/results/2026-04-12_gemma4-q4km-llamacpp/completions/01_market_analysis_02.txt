**Signal Decomposition:**

1. **2s10s yield spread at +22 bps after -108 bps inversion:** The full un-inversion is the most important signal. Murphy's framework and the broader credit cycle model show that the recession typically arrives 6–18 months after re-steepening. The inversion itself was the warning; the un-inversion is the implementation phase. We are roughly 3–6 months into that window.

2. **Fed Funds at 4.50% with core PCE at 2.8%:** This is the critical constraint. The Fed has limited room to cut rates without risking inflation re-acceleration. This means the tightening cycle's effects will continue to transmit through the economy without the easing that historically cushions the recessionary transition. The 2000–2002 recession was accompanied by Fed cuts; the 2001–2003 recession saw cuts that were delayed and insufficient.

3. **ISM Manufacturing PMI at 48.3:** Below 50 for the first time in months, confirming manufacturing contraction. This is a leading indicator that precedes broader economic weakening.

4. **JOLTS ratio from 2.0x to 1.1x:** The labor market is cooling but not yet in freefall. The 1.1x ratio still shows more jobs than unemployed workers, which is a key difference from the 2008 crisis when the ratio fell below 0.5x. This suggests the labor market is weakening but hasn't reached the breaking point.

**Credit Cycle Phase Identification:**

Applying the credit cycle framework, we are in the late-cycle transition phase. The un-inversion signals that the credit cycle has peaked and is turning. The ISM contraction confirms the downturn has begun in the manufacturing sector. The JOLTS ratio shows the labor market is weakening but not yet in crisis. The PCE at 2.8% with Fed Funds at 4.50% creates a policy constraint that limits the Fed's ability to cushion the transition. This is the most dangerous phase because it's when the recession is most likely to be deeper than expected — the tightening is already in place, but the easing hasn't arrived.

**Equity Sector Implications:**

Historically, during the late-cycle to early-recession transition, defensive sectors outperform. Consumer staples, utilities, healthcare, and low-beta financials tend to hold up better. The S&P 500's defensive sector rotation rule suggests rotating out of cyclical sectors (technology, industrials, consumer discretionary) and into defensive sectors. Specifically, the consumer staples sector has historically outperformed the S&P 500 by 8–12% during the first 6 months of recessionary periods.

**Soft-Landing Conditions That Would Change the Thesis:**

Three conditions would shift this toward a soft-landing thesis: (1) the JOLTS ratio stabilizes above 1.0x for 3+ months, suggesting the labor market has found a floor; (2) the ISM PMI reverses above 50, indicating manufacturing is bottoming; and (3) core PCE declines below 2.3%, giving the Fed room to cut rates without inflation risk. If all three occur simultaneously, the recession thesis weakens substantially.
