This scenario directly invokes Aronson's chapter on data mining and the statistical method, where he warns that the more parameters you test, the more likely you are to find a strategy that appears to work purely by chance. The analyst has run 847 parameter combinations — that's a massive search space, and the risk of data-mining bias is substantial.

**Data-Mining Bias Quantification:** Aronson's framework provides a method to estimate the expected best-Sharpe from pure noise. With 847 independent trials, the expected maximum Sharpe ratio from random chance alone is approximately 1.60–1.70. This is calculated using the distribution of the maximum of N independent normal variables. The analyst's reported Sharpe of 1.84 is only modestly above what pure noise would produce — it's not far enough above the noise floor to be convincing without further validation. The 847 combinations are likely not independent — many parameter combinations are highly correlated (e.g., a 12/48 EMA crossover is very similar to a 13/49 EMA crossover), so the effective number of independent trials is probably closer to 100–200, which would lower the noise-floor estimate to roughly 1.30–1.40. Even so, 1.84 vs. 1.40 is not a decisive margin.

**White's Reality Check and Bootstrap Procedures:** Before presenting to investors, the analyst must apply White's Reality Check or a bootstrap-based method to determine whether the observed strategy's performance exceeds what would be expected from random chance. White's Reality Check (as described in Aronson's chapter) involves: (1) computing the best-in-sample Sharpe ratio, (2) generating a large number of random strategies (e.g., 10,000) and computing their best-in-sample Sharpes, (3) constructing the distribution of these maximums, and (4) determining the p-value — the proportion of random strategies whose best-in-sample Sharpe exceeds the observed 1.84. A p-value below 0.05 would be the minimum threshold for confidence. Alternatively, a bootstrap method would involve resampling the original data with replacement, running the optimization on each bootstrap sample, and comparing the out-of-sample performance of the original best-in-sample strategy to the distribution of out-of-sample performances from the bootstrap runs.

**Walk-Forward Methodology:** The analyst should implement a walk-forward optimization (WFO) procedure. Split the 2010–2024 data into in-sample and out-of-sample periods. For example, use 2010–2018 as in-sample (training) and 2019–2024 as out-of-sample (testing). Run the same 847-combination optimization on the in-sample period, identify the best parameters, and then evaluate those parameters on the out-of-sample period. A minimum out-of-sample test length of 3 years is recommended to capture diverse market regimes. The out-of-sample Sharpe should ideally exceed 1.00 and the maximum drawdown should be within 50% of the in-sample drawdown of -14.2%. If the out-of-sample Sharpe drops below 0.50 or the drawdown exceeds -25%, the strategy is likely overfit and should not be presented to investors.
