This trader is exhibiting the disposition effect — the tendency to sell winners too early and hold losers too long — which is one of the most well-documented and costly behavioral biases in trading. The evidence is unambiguous: holding times of 4.2 days for winners versus 11.7 days for losers (a 2.8x difference), a win rate of 62% that fails to produce positive expectancy, and the explicit observation of 14 missed opportunities where profits ran an additional 15–30% after he exited. The negative expectancy of -$77 per trade despite a 62% win rate is the smoking gun — his edge is being destroyed by asymmetric holding.

Shefrin and Statman's disposition effect framework, as synthesized by Marks and others, explains this through the prospect theory utility function. The key insight is that traders evaluate gains and losses asymmetrically: the disutility of a realized loss is greater than the disutility of a foregone gain, and the utility of a realized gain is less than the utility of an unrealized gain. This creates two distinct behaviors. First, when a trade is in the gain zone, the trader's utility function is concave — he prefers the certain gain of selling now over the uncertain larger gain of holding. This explains why he exits winners after only 4.2 days. Second, when a trade is in the loss zone, the utility function is convex — he prefers the uncertain gamble of holding (hoping for recovery) over the certain loss of selling. This explains the 11.7-day average hold on losers. The 14 missed opportunities where prices ran 15–30% further are the cost of the first behavior. The negative expectancy of -$77 per trade is the cost of the second.

The deeper problem is that this trader's decision-making is being driven by the emotional utility function described by Kahneman and Tversky, not by a rational expected-value framework. His 62% win rate suggests he has genuine market insight — his directional calls are correct more than 50% of the time — but his execution is destroying the edge. This is a particularly frustrating scenario because the trader has the harder part (market insight) but is failing at the easier part (trade management).

The structured protocol to correct this must remove the discretionary element from the exit decision entirely. The trader should implement a 3-rule systematic exit framework: (1) Hard stop-loss at a pre-defined level (e.g., 1.5x ATR below entry) entered as a conditional order at the same time as the buy order, so the decision is made before the trade begins; (2) Trailing stop-loss that tightens as the trade moves in his favor, replacing the concave utility function's preference for certain small gains with a systematic method for capturing larger gains; (3) A profit target rule that triggers a partial exit at 2R but leaves 50% of the position to run with the trailing stop, preventing the all-or-nothing decision that drives the disposition effect. The trader should also compute his expected value per trade using the 62% win rate and the new systematic exit parameters before each trade, and only take trades with a positive expected value. This transforms the decision from emotional utility maximization to expected value maximization.
