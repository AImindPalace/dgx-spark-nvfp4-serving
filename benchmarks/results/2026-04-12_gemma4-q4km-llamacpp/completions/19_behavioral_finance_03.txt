This scenario is a textbook example of what Duke's framework calls 'resulting' — judging the quality of a position by its outcome rather than its process — and what Tetlock would call a hedgehog-style oversimplification masquerading as a fox-style probability estimate. The strategist has assigned a 25% probability to recession, which sounds moderate, but her portfolio is positioned for a 70% equity allocation that would suffer a 25% loss in that scenario. Let me decompose this using a probability-weighted expected value method.

First, the expected portfolio return: (0.25 × -25%) + (0.55 × +12%) + (0.20 × +20%) = -6.25% + 6.6% + 4.0% = +4.35% expected return. At first glance, the expected return is positive, which might give the strategist false confidence. But this is the expected value, not the probability-weighted downside risk — and the latter is far more concerning.

The probability-weighted downside risk is 0.25 × -25% = -6.25% expected loss from the recession scenario alone. The portfolio's 70% equity allocation means that in the 25% of scenarios where recession occurs, the portfolio is taking a hit that is disproportionate to the probability assigned. Duke's resulting trap is active here: the strategist likely feels comfortable because 'recession is only 25% likely,' but she's not evaluating whether her portfolio's sensitivity to that 25% scenario is appropriate. This is exactly the error Duke warns about — judging a decision by its outcome rather than by the process of matching position sizing to probability estimates.

Applying Tetlock's fox-versus-hedgehog framework: the strategist's 25/55/20 probability estimate is a hedgehog-style single-scenario anchor. She's assigned a probability but hasn't built a portfolio that reflects the full distribution. A true fox would ask: 'What is the portfolio's expected value across all scenarios, and does it match my probability-weighted expectations?' The current portfolio has an expected return of +4.35% but a 25% chance of a -25% drawdown — that's a skewed distribution with fat tail risk.

The restructuring recommendation is to align portfolio sensitivity with probability estimates. If recession is 25% likely and would cause a 25% loss, the equity allocation should be reduced to a level where the expected loss from that scenario is acceptable relative to the overall portfolio. A more appropriate allocation would be approximately 50% equities, which would change the expected portfolio return to: (0.25 × -17.5%) + (0.55 × +8.4%) + (0.20 × +14%) = -4.375% + 4.62% + 2.8% = +3.045% expected return with a much smaller downside contribution from the recession scenario. This is a less aggressive but more robust position that reflects the true probability distribution.

The key takeaway from Duke's framework: the strategist should evaluate her portfolio construction by asking whether the position sizing matches her probability estimates, not whether the expected return is positive. A positive expected return with a 25% chance of a 25% loss is not 'hedged' — it's a bet with fat-tail risk that she's underestimating because she's anchored to the 25% probability figure.
