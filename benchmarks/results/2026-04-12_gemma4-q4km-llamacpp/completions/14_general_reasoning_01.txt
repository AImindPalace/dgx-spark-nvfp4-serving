This is a sophisticated question that requires synthesizing the Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox, Aronson's epistemological critique of technical analysis, and Dalio's principles of radical truth-seeking into a coherent framework for determining research intensity.

The Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox (1980) states that if markets were perfectly efficient, no one would have an incentive to analyze, prices would become uninformative, and markets would become inefficient — creating a profitable opportunity for active managers. But if active managers exploit this inefficiency, they drive prices toward efficiency, reducing their own edge. The equilibrium is a market that is 'just inefficient enough' to compensate active managers for their research costs — not perfectly efficient, but not so inefficient that obvious arbitrage persists. This is a fundamental insight: the existence of a profitable active management strategy implies that the market is not perfectly efficient, and the size of that inefficiency must at least cover the cost of the research required to exploit it.

Aronson's epistemological critique adds a critical layer. He argues that most technical analysis is based on a flawed assumption — that patterns in historical price data have predictive power. His research showed that when you properly account for data-mining bias, the vast majority of 'statistically significant' technical patterns disappear. This means that for most discretionary traders, the pattern-seeking they're doing is actually data-mining without awareness. The 'patterns' they see are artifacts of their search process, not genuine market signals. This is a direct application of Dalio's principle of radical truth-seeking: the most uncomfortable truth — that your patterns may be illusory — is the one you most need to confront.

The synthesis of these three frameworks produces a concrete decision rule for research intensity. For a discretionary trader managing $5M, the Grossman-Stiglitz equilibrium implies that genuine alpha opportunities exist but are likely small and fleeting. The Aronson critique suggests that most pattern-based research will be illusory. The Dalio truth-seeking principle demands you explicitly test your patterns for data-mining bias before acting on them. The appropriate research intensity is moderate but focused: spend 70% of research time on hypothesis testing (is this pattern real?) and 30% on implementation (how do I trade it?). The target is to identify 2-3 genuinely robust patterns that survive rigorous testing and trade them with disciplined risk management.

For a quant fund managing $5B, the situation is different. The Grossman-Stiglitz equilibrium implies that the fund must continuously innovate to maintain its edge, because its very presence in the market is driving prices toward efficiency. The Aronson critique is even more relevant because a quant fund's data-mining risk is enormous — with thousands of hypotheses tested daily, the probability of finding spurious patterns approaches 100%. The appropriate research intensity is high but must be accompanied by rigorous out-of-sample testing, cross-validation, and transaction cost modeling. The target is to identify 50-100 hypotheses per year, with only 5-10 surviving to production.

The key failure condition is assuming that any pattern you find is real without explicitly testing for data-mining bias. This is the most common and most expensive mistake in discretionary trading.
